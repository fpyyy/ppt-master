# Literature Review on Face Liveness Detection: System Applications, Mainstream Methods, and Frontier Progress

Face liveness detection (Face Anti-Spoofing, FAS), also frequently referred to as Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) in both academia and industry, serves as the core defense line ensuring the security of modern face recognition systems. Although face recognition algorithms based on deep learning have reached or even surpassed human-level accuracy in unconstrained identity matching in recent years, these systems still exhibit significant vulnerabilities when confronted with maliciously forged biometric presentation attacks. System attackers typically attempt to deceive authentication protocols by presenting forged biometric carriers (i.e., Presentation Attack Instruments, PAIs) to camera sensors, thereby illegally gaining system access privileges.

From the fundamental logic of computer vision and pattern recognition, face liveness detection is primarily modeled as a binary classification problem. Its core objective is to learn and deploy a highly robust decision function to accurately distinguish whether the samples captured by the camera originate from a genuine live subject (Live) or a forged attack (Spoof). Before the widespread adoption of deep learning, early research heavily relied on handcrafted feature descriptors, such as Local Binary Patterns (LBP), Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG), and Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT), aiming to extract local texture anomalies and frequency artifacts from images. However, with increasingly realistic attack methods and more complex application scenarios, traditional feature-based approaches can no longer meet practical demands. Over the past five years (2021-2026), cutting-edge methods based on deep learning—particularly Vision Transformers, cross-domain multi-modal learning, generative Diffusion Models, and Vision-Language Prompting—have completely reshaped the research paradigm in this field. This report will systematically outline system application instances and system attack taxonomies of face liveness detection, deeply review mainstream and highly effective research methods published in top computer vision journals and conferences (such as CVPR, ICCV, ECCV, TPAMI) in recent years, explicitly highlight open-source research achievements, and detail the State-of-the-Art (SOTA) levels currently attained in this domain.

## System Application Instances of Face Liveness Detection

The application scenarios of face liveness detection technologies are closely tied to their hardware constraints, threat models, and security level requirements. When deployed in practice, systems must strike a delicate balance among detection accuracy, inference latency, computational resources, and user interaction experience. Currently, face liveness detection systems are widely deployed and play an irreplaceable role in the following key sectors.

### Mobile and Personal Smart Device Authentication

From face unlocking on smartphones to system logins on personal laptops, mobile devices have become the most ubiquitous application terminals for face recognition technology. In these scenarios, liveness detection systems typically face severely constrained computational resources, uncontrollable environmental illumination changes (e.g., strong backlighting or extremely dark environments), and very short inference time windows. Since the 2D imaging characteristics of pure RGB cameras easily fail against high-resolution screen replays, modern mobile liveness detection systems increasingly rely on built-in multi-modal sensor arrays. By fusing standard RGB images, Depth maps generated by structured light or Time-of-Flight (ToF), and infrared spectral images captured by Infrared (IR) sensors, systems can extract richer anti-spoofing cues in a multi-dimensional feature space, thereby achieving high-precision real-time attack interception on low-compute platforms.

### Financial Services and Electronic Identity Verification (eKYC)

High-risk operations in the financial sector, such as remote account opening, large-amount transfer authorization, and online lending, heavily rely on electronic "Know Your Customer" (eKYC) protocols. In this application instance, systems face extremely high financial fraud risks, and attackers often have ample resources to fabricate high-fidelity silicone masks or employ advanced Deepfake technologies for digital video injection attacks. Therefore, financial-grade applications typically adopt a combination of passive and active detection strategies. The system not only silently analyzes spatial pixel anomalies, spectral distortions, and frequency mutations in the video stream in the background, but also prompts users with random instructions to perform specific actions (such as blinking, shaking the head, opening the mouth, or reading randomly generated numeric CAPTCHAs). This verifies that the user is not only a physically present live subject but also possesses real-time response capabilities at the moment of authentication.

### Physical Access Control and National Border Security Checks

At airport boarding gates, automated border control gates (e-Gates), and access control systems for high-security facilities, face liveness detection plays a crucial role in preventing illegal physical intrusions. These scenarios are characterized by high throughput and dense pedestrian flow, requiring the authentication process to be seamless, rapid, and highly accurate. Although lighting conditions in these environments are relatively fixed and controllable, their threat models include highly motivated professional attackers who might use customized 3D resin masks or high-precision casting tools for physical identity theft. To counter such advanced threats, supplementary liveness detection solutions based on acoustic signals have emerged in recent years, such as the Echo-FAS system. By emitting sonar-like audio pulses and analyzing their echo characteristics, this system can infer the 3D structural information of the target face with very low hardware costs, constructing another solid anti-spoofing barrier alongside traditional optical sensors.

## System Attack Instances: Taxonomy of Presentation Attacks and Digital Forgeries

To construct an extremely robust defense system, it is vital to deeply deconstruct and understand the various attack vectors faced by face recognition systems. The current threat landscape can be broadly divided into two major camps: Physical Presentation Attacks and Digital Editing Attacks. Recent major academic challenges (such as the related competitions in CVPR 2024 and ICCV 2025) are dedicated to detecting these two distinctly different attack modes simultaneously within a unified framework.

### Physical Presentation Attacks

Physical attacks refer to attackers presenting a physically existing, artificially fabricated fake physical object to the camera sensor. Based on the different physical properties of the presentation mediums, such attacks can be subdivided into the following primary forms:

Print Attacks are the most basic and lowest-cost attack method, where attackers directly present a 2D printed photograph of the target identity in front of the camera. Such attacks range from presenting flat photos, intentionally distorting or bending photos to simulate facial 3D curvature, to cut-out attacks where holes are cut in the eye regions of the photo to reveal the attacker's real eyes, thereby deceiving blink detection algorithms. In print attacks, the main forgery cues that systems can capture include color distortions caused by printer gamut limitations, the lack of natural 3D depth information of a real face, and the absence of microscopic textures and subsurface reflection characteristics unique to human skin on the paper surface.

Video Replay Attacks utilize tablets, smartphones, or high-resolution LCD monitors to play pre-recorded videos of the target identity to the system. This attack can easily bypass many early liveness detection systems based on simple motion analysis. The core anti-spoofing cues here are mainly concentrated on the physical properties of the digital screens themselves, including Moiré patterns generated by the interference between the screen pixel grid and the camera sensor pixel grid, unnatural specular reflections on the screen glass surface, inevitable exposure of screen bezels, and the lack of remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) physiological signal responses unique to biological organisms on replay devices.

3D Mask Attacks represent the most technically challenging category of physical presentation attacks. Attackers use materials like silicone, plastic, plaster, or customized paper models to create target masks with precise 3D geometric structures. Because such masks possess genuine 3D topological attributes, traditional depth-based anti-spoofing methods relying on multi-view stereo or structured light ranging often risk failure. However, since the manufacturing materials of these non-biological attributes cannot replicate the physiological and optical properties of real human tissues (such as the faint blood flow in subcutaneous capillaries, the diffuse reflection of the dermis, and the subtle dynamic changes of facial muscles), deep learning models based on fine-grained multi-spectral analysis or physiological signal extraction have become the most effective weapons for defending against such advanced attacks.

### Digital Forgery and Editing Attacks

Unlike physical attacks that alter optical presentations at the sensor frontend, digital editing attacks inject or tamper with pixel-level data directly within the digital image processing pipeline. This category encompasses Deepfakes generated by Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) or Diffusion Models, real-time face-swapping video streams, and adversarial noise attacks designed against neural network vulnerabilities. In digital forgery scenarios, the nature of anti-spoofing cues fundamentally changes. They no longer manifest as flaws in physical media but rather appear as unnatural high-frequency mutations in the frequency domain space, abrupt blending boundaries between generated regions and the background, and anomalous pixel value sets that significantly deviate from the natural image manifold distribution of real faces.

### The Rise of Unified Attack Detection (UAD)

In past research, physical presentation attack detection and digital deepfake detection were often advanced separately as two isolated computer vision subfields. However, the incompatibility of such detection algorithms in real-world deployments has left severe protection blind spots for security systems. To address this pain point, recent academic focus (such as the Unified Physical-Digital Attack Detection challenges held at CVPR 2024 and ICCV 2025) strongly advocates exploring and extracting the commonalities of these two attack methods in terms of low-level visual features. Researchers have discovered that whether it is color attenuation caused by physical media material limitations or pixel interpolation anomalies caused during digital editing, both inherently disrupt the natural continuity and high-low frequency distribution characteristics intrinsic to authentic facial images. Therefore, building a unified architecture capable of leveraging global attention mechanisms to simultaneously capture multiple anomaly cues represents the foremost evolutionary direction in this field.

## Research Status: Mainstream Research Methods and Literature Review

Over the past five years, academic research in the face liveness detection domain has transitioned comprehensively from Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) extracting local features to complex multi-modal fusion, Domain Generalization (DG), Vision-Language Large Model prompt learning, and generative paradigms. The biggest technical bottleneck currently faced is the "Domain Shift" problem: models trained on a specific dataset (source domain) often experience a precipitous drop in performance when encountering entirely new deployment environments, unknown camera sensor models, or novel attack mediums (target domain). Addressing this pain point, literature from top journals and conferences has proposed various highly innovative solutions.

### 1. Multi-Modal Learning and Modality Rebalancing

Given that uni-modal RGB sensors are highly susceptible to complex environmental illumination interference and struggle to resist high-fidelity print or replay attacks, expanding the model input from a single RGB channel to a multi-modal format including Depth and Infrared (IR) has become the mainstream engineering practice for capturing multi-semantic anti-spoofing cues. However, in complex cross-domain generalization scenarios, multi-modal learning introduces two extremely challenging optimization problems: Modality Unreliability and Modality Imbalance.

To thoroughly resolve these issues, the top conference paper "Suppress and Rebalance: Towards Generalized Multi-Modal Face Anti-Spoofing" (**(https://github.com/OMGGGGG/mmdg)]**) published at CVPR 2024 proposed an innovative framework named Multi-Modal Domain Generalized (MMDG). Firstly, addressing Modality Unreliability: when the system is deployed in an unknown testing environment, or the target domain uses depth or infrared imaging sensors of different models than those in the training set, severe domain shifts occur. This shift causes the network to extract highly unreliable spoof features, inevitably polluting other originally healthy modalities during the cross-modal feature fusion stage. The MMDG framework introduces an Uncertainty-Guided Cross-Adapter (U-Adapter) as a Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning (PETL) mechanism for Vision Transformers (ViT). The U-Adapter evaluates the uncertainty of each image patch feature in real-time via Monte Carlo sampling technology. Once it detects that the features of a certain region are highly unstable, the U-Adapter adaptively suppresses the system's focus on these unreliable tokens during attention-based cross-modal fusion, strictly preventing contaminated spoof cues from propagating through the network and preserving the model's overall discriminative purity. Secondly, addressing Modality Imbalance: during joint multi-modal optimization, the network tends to overly rely on the dominant modality with the fastest convergence speed (usually the RGB modality, i.e., the "fast modality"), causing the feature representation capabilities of other slower-converging modalities (like Depth or IR, i.e., the "slow modalities") to remain under-exploited. If the target domain contains specific attacks that render the fast modality ineffective, the under-optimized slow modalities fail to provide adequate supplementary defense. To break this optimization bias, researchers proposed a strategy called Rebalanced Modality Gradient Modulation (ReGrad). The ReGrad module continuously monitors the convergence speeds of each modality and the degree of conflict among them during training. Based on this, it dynamically and adaptively adjusts the magnitude of gradients allocated to all trainable parameters in the U-Adapters during backpropagation. This extremely fine-grained gradient intervention mechanism forces the network to fully utilize the potential of all modalities, significantly enhancing the comprehensive generalization capabilities of multi-modal liveness detection models in unseen environments.

### 2. Deep Disentanglement of Physiological Feature Cues and Remote Photoplethysmography (rPPG)

A live human face harbors a series of microscopic physiological responses that non-living materials cannot simulate. Remote Photoplethysmography (rPPG) technology provides a biologically grounded discriminative method for liveness detection by analyzing the faint color fluctuations on the human skin surface caused by the cardiac cycle and subcutaneous blood volume changes. Since physical forged masks and static paper photos absolutely possess no pulse heartbeat, the rPPG signal naturally serves as a powerful, intrinsic expression of liveness features.

However, extracting pure and continuous rPPG signals in uncontrolled real-world environments is highly challenging due to intertwined specular and diffuse reflections caused by drastic head movements and rapid environmental illumination changes. Recent research literature (e.g., works published at ICCV 2025) focuses on fundamentally disentangling rPPG signals from motion noise. Researchers innovatively introduced the Generalizable Gaussian Model (GGM), utilizing 4D Gaussian representation technology to explicitly separate the spatial geometric features of the face from its time-varying chroma components. To overcome the universal lack of camera parameters in datasets, which prevents direct video rendering from 4D Gaussians, this method creatively proposed a "4D virtual camera" architecture, constructing additional parameters to detail perspective changes and motion trajectories. Based on this architecture, researchers designed Explicit Motion Modeling (EMM) to strip away the physical displacement of head pose in an unsupervised manner, supplemented by Explicit Chroma Modeling (ECM) to accurately eliminate environmental specular reflections and sensor noise signals, thereby restoring the most authentic physiological pulse fluctuations. By feeding this purified rPPG signal as a time-series feature into a hybrid CNN-RNN network for auxiliary supervised learning, modern models demonstrate outstanding detection stability against high-fidelity 3D headgears and mask attacks without sacrificing spatial texture recognition accuracy.

### 3. Vision Transformer (ViT) Architectures and Vision-Language Large Model Guidance

Constrained by their local receptive fields, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) often overly rely on texture anomalies in specific regions, leading to insufficient generalization when facing novel attacks. In contrast, Vision Transformer (ViT) models, through their core global self-attention mechanisms, can acutely capture long-range global dependencies between image patches, demonstrating immense architectural advantages in discovering spatially widespread, subtle deepfake cues.

However, fully unlocking ViT's potential for the liveness detection task requires far more than merely using weights pre-trained on ImageNet. Breakthrough progress over the past two years involves introducing Vision-Language Pre-training (VLP) models (like CLIP) into the liveness detection domain to provide higher-dimensional semantic alignment guidance. The focal paper "FLIP: Cross-domain Face Anti-spoofing with Language Guidance" (**(https://github.com/koushiksrivats/FLIP)]**) published at ICCV 2023 was the first to demonstrate that initializing ViTs with multi-modal pre-trained weights can significantly improve the zero-shot generalization capabilities of models in FAS tasks. The FLIP framework proposes a cross-domain method utilizing natural language to assist visual representations. Specifically, rather than solely relying on image pixel features, this method forces the model's visual feature encodings to align in a multi-modal hyperspace with a series of category descriptions constructed based on natural language semantics (e.g., the text prompt "A fake face photo presenting a print attack") via a multi-modal contrastive learning strategy. This semantic-based guidance mechanism greatly enhances feature abstract generalization, enabling the model to understand the essence of "forgery" just as it understands linguistic concepts, thereby achieving astonishing cross-domain transfer performance in few-shot or even zero-shot scenarios. Following closely, "CFPL-FAS: Class Free Prompt Learning for Generalizable Face Anti-spoofing" published at CVPR 2024 further expanded on this concept. To eliminate reliance on predefined attack category labels, CFPL-FAS proposes a class-free prompt learning framework. By introducing a Style Prompt Diversification mechanism during the training phase, this method utilizes a minimal number of trainable parameters to drive frozen large vision foundation models, forcing them to extract robust, broadly applicable domain-agnostic anti-spoofing features even without explicit knowledge of specific attack types. Furthermore, addressing the issue of disconnected evaluation metrics, the ICCV 2025 study "One Model, All Attacks: ACER-Optimized Vision Transformer" points out that traditional cross-entropy loss functions are not directly optimized for the official evaluation standards of biometric security. Therefore, researchers proposed a single-stream ViT network trained with a hinge-ACER loss function. During backpropagation, this network directly takes the minimization of the Average Classification Error Rate (ACER) as the optimization goal. Combined with attack-type data augmentation strategies specifically designed for color distortions, optical reflections, and motion blur, it not only reduces the model's reliance on specific identity features but also trains a formidable defender capable of unified resistance against physical and digital composite attacks.

### 4. Domain Generalization (DG) and Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)

Since the data distribution of the target deployment environment in actual face liveness detection applications often exhibits unpredictable and massive discrepancies from that of the source training set, merely relying on extracting "domain-invariant" features on the source domain cannot guarantee absolute security. Thus, Domain Generalization (DG) technologies and Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) mechanisms have become key research directions to bridge this gap.

The "Test-Time Domain Generalization" (TTDG) framework proposed at CVPR 2024 subverts traditional inference modes. The core insight of the TTDG methodology is that test data in the target domain inherently contains highly valuable distribution information; it should not merely be treated as an input object for evaluation but should serve as high-quality resources to further enhance the model's generalization. By utilizing unlabeled test samples in real-time during the inference phase for self-supervised exploration or gradient-based dynamic feature alignment, FAS models can instantly and adaptively fine-tune their decision boundaries based on specific environmental lighting or unknown sensor characteristics encountered at that moment, greatly increasing interception success rates under sudden extreme shift environments. On the other hand, in "BUDoPT: Bottom-Up Domain Prompt Tuning for Generalized Face Anti-Spoofing" published at ECCV 2024, researchers attempted to proactively peel off domain information through prompt learning. This method extracts generalized Domain Prompts via a bottom-up visual encoder, subsequently introducing an Adversarial Domain Prompt Learning strategy. By explicitly minimizing domain-specific traces in the text embedding space, this strategy applies reverse pressure on the visual backbone network, forcing it to filter out irrelevant confounding variables (such as camera models or background lighting) at the very forefront of feature extraction, thereby distilling exceptionally pure high-frequency discriminative features sensitive only to forgery attributes.

### 5. Revolutionary Applications of Generative Diffusion Models in Anti-Spoofing (DiffFAS)

In recent years, the most profound paradigm shift in face anti-spoofing literature has undeniably been the leap from purely discriminative boundary demarcation to generative distribution modeling. Traditional discriminative models heavily rely on limited training samples and predefined attack classes to delineate hyperplanes, rendering them often helpless when encountering novel out-of-distribution (OOD) attacks. Generative models, however, attempt to fundamentally understand the intrinsic logic of image generation by estimating the latent probability density functions of real or forged faces.

The paper "DiffFAS: Face Anti-Spoofing via Generative Diffusion Models" published at the top conference ECCV 2024 is a representative masterpiece of this revolutionary direction. The study re-examines the nature of "domain shift" that causes model generalization failures, precisely deconstructing it into two independent core factors: Image Style (i.e., the specific way spoof information is presented, influenced by lighting and camera sensor noise) and Image Quality (i.e., the purity and clarity of the spoof cues). To combat shifts brought by Image Quality, DiffFAS abandons simple binary classification losses and proposes a Sample-Level Relative Quality loss. This method ingeniously inputs professional no-reference image quality assessment scores (like BRISQUE scores) into the network as prior knowledge. By modifying the Softmax loss function with angular or additive margins, it forces the network to understand low-quality (LQ) image degradation features caused by physical recapturing or poor printing, thereby learning an extremely compact quality-aware feature cluster. To combat shifts brought by Image Style, DiffFAS completely reconstructs the generative process of diffusion models, granting them high-fidelity, cross-domain, and cross-attack type sample generation capabilities. In practice, this method adopts an image-editing sampling strategy: it first injects $t$ steps of random noise into a real live image to erase its original domain style, and then, based on specific conditional guidance, performs reverse diffusion sampling from this noisy latent variable to generate entirely new images. This process astonishingly translates easily collectible ordinary live faces into highly realistic fake samples that possess strict identity consistency but exhibit various unknown attack styles (e.g., screen replays under different lighting, prints of different materials). Such generative data augmentation techniques, synthesizing unseen target domain data "out of thin air" during the training phase via diffusion models, immensely alleviate the scarcity of labeled data and drastically reduce model error rates in cross-domain testing scenarios. Sharing a similar philosophy is the **FaceSpoofLDM** system based on Latent Diffusion Models. Utilizing language guidance mechanisms (such as cross-attention), this system can generate an unlimited supply of realistic presentation attack training samples by inputting text prompts that describe the attack medium types (e.g., silicone masks, paper photos) and soft biometrics (e.g., age, gender, ethnicity). This training dataset, synthesized entirely by artificial intelligence, not only significantly enhances cross-domain liveness detection performance but also fundamentally circumvents the severe privacy and data compliance risks triggered by collecting massive amounts of real human face attack data.

## Current Achieved Levels: Multi-Dimensional Cross-Domain Benchmark Quantitative Analysis and SOTA Comparison

Evaluating the actual effectiveness of face liveness detection algorithms relies on a series of standardized, rigorous testing protocols. Given that mainstream deep learning models generally achieve extremely high or even saturated accuracy in intra-domain testing (intra-dataset evaluation, where training and testing occur within the same dataset), today's top academic conferences have shifted their evaluation focus almost entirely toward cross-domain testing protocols that truly reflect a system's generalization capabilities. Among them, the Leave-One-Dataset-Out (LODO) cross-dataset evaluation protocol is the ultimate touchstone for measuring whether algorithms can survive in the unknown real world.

### Core Benchmark Dataset Overview

The most authoritative cross-domain evaluation benchmark in academia is commonly known as the **OCIM protocol**, which is built upon four mainstream 2D physical attack datasets with distinct feature differences :

1. **OULU-NPU (O):** Highly challenging, containing 4,950 videos of 55 subjects. Its core characteristic is that all data are captured by 6 different mobile devices under three distinct lighting environments (Sessions), heavily testing the model's generalizability against heterogeneous camera hardware and complex illumination.
2. **CASIA-FASD (C):** Contains 600 videos of 50 subjects. Its primary design variable is video definition quality, categorized into low resolution (640×480, acquired by old long-used USB cameras), normal quality (acquired by new USB cameras), and high resolution, covering common print and screen replay attacks.
3. **Idiap Replay-Attack (I):** Contains 1,200 videos of 50 subjects. It focuses on evaluating anti-spoofing performance under controlled and adverse lighting conditions, mainly featuring high-definition screen replays on tablets and smartphones of varying sizes.
4. **MSU-MFSD (M):** Contains 280 videos of 35 subjects. Collected via Google Nexus 5 phones and MacBook Air laptop cameras, its attack types include exceptionally high-fidelity video replays on iPad Air and iPhone 5S, alongside high-resolution print photo attacks.

Beyond these four fundamental physical attack datasets, for multi-modal liveness detection and cutting-edge comprehensive defense verification, academia also utilizes massive datasets like CASIA-SURF CeFA (multi-modal cross-ethnicity), WMCA and PADISI (focusing on multiple types of 3D masks and disguise glasses), and the UniAttackDataPlus unified dataset introduced in the CVPR 2024 challenges, which mixes physical attacks with digital editing tampering tests.

### Evaluation Metrics Definition

During the quantitative analysis phase, the core evaluation metrics recognized by the community primarily include the **Half Total Error Rate (HTER)** and the **Area Under Curve (AUC) of the Receiver Operating Characteristic**.

- **HTER:** Calculated as the arithmetic mean of the False Positive Rate (misclassifying an attack as a live subject) and the False Negative Rate (falsely rejecting a true live subject as an attack). A lower value indicates a smaller comprehensive probability of false interceptions and false acceptances, implying higher absolute system security.
- **AUC:** Represents the model's comprehensive discrimination capability across different classification thresholds. A value closer to 100% indicates a stronger ability to differentiate between live and spoof features, signifying a smoother and more robust decision hyperplane.
- Some studies focusing on industrial deployment standards also report **ACER (Average Classification Error Rate)** and the Bona fide Presentation Classification Error Rate (BPCER) at a fixed Attack Presentation Classification Error Rate (e.g., APCER=1%). This is particularly critical for balancing user experience with the rigid demands of financial-grade security systems.

### In-Depth Perspective and Status Summary of Quantitative Results

By deeply analyzing the above benchmark comparison data, one can clearly observe the extraordinary leaps achieved by face liveness detection technologies within just a few years. Between 2020 and 2022, early purely discriminative domain generalization networks (such as SSDG-M and SSAN) still hovered in the dangerous zone of 15% to 20% for comprehensive average HTER in cross-domain testing. This indicates that mainstream algorithms at the time overfitted to the specific optical defects of source dataset camera sensors, causing their error rates to spike sharply past 23% when facing distinct high-contrast replay attacks, such as those unique to the MSU dataset.

However, entering 2023 and 2024, with the comprehensive penetration of Transformer architectures and Generative AI, technical bottlenecks have been thoroughly shattered. Multi-modal prompt learning methods, represented by FLIP-MCL, hard-mapped low-level visual fake pixels into abstract natural language description spaces, vastly enhancing the semantic generalizability of features and directly plummeting the average HTER precipitously to 3.01%. The most advanced works from ECCV 2024, TF-FAS and DiffFAS-V, relying on fine-grained semantic dual guidance and diffusion-based data generation augmentation respectively, firmly compressed the comprehensive average HTER to extreme levels between 1% and 6% under tremendously difficult cross-validation protocols, with corresponding AUCs nearly topping out at 99.90%. This remarkable achievement sufficiently proves that modern algorithms based on large models and generative paradigms can already achieve near-perfect zero-shot generalization capabilities across the vast majority of standard 2D physical attack scenarios.

In even more stringent multi-modal detection and sensor-missing scenarios, the MMDG (Suppress and Rebalance) model published at CVPR 2024 similarly showcased dominating performance. When undergoing cross-domain evaluations on massive multi-modal data like CASIA-CeFA, PADISI, CASIA-SURF, and WMCA, the MMDG framework successfully pushed the absolute HTER value of traditional multi-modal CNN baselines down by a further 2.19% and boosted the AUC by 2.71% by dynamically suppressing unreliable infrared or depth features. This proves that even in extremely adverse environments where individual physical sensors fail or suffer from high-intensity interference, the system's defenses remain impregnable through ingenious gradient rebalancing mechanisms. Simultaneously, addressing mixed testing environments of digital editing and deepfake attacks, the newly optimized single-stream ViT networks have achieved ACERs below 8% on immensely complex, ultra-large datasets like UniAttackDataPlus , signaling that the grand unification of physical and digital anti-spoofing in algorithmic architecture is accelerating towards mature application.

## Conclusion and Frontier Development Assessment

In summary, over the past five years of research, face liveness detection technology has completed a fundamental revolution in underlying architecture and methodology. Based on a comprehensive and detailed review alongside a forward-looking analysis of current literature data, several clear assessments regarding the future developmental trajectory of this field can be made.

First, the traditional mindset of treating face anti-spoofing as a static, closed-environment, binary feature extraction problem has been entirely discarded by academia. The evolution of physical materials in the real world (such as increasingly thin and realistic silicone skins) and the open-sourcing of deepfake algorithms have caused intra-class variances of presentation attacks to grow exponentially. SOTA data from literature irrefutably proves that relying merely on traditional networks to extract and align limited "domain-invariant" features will inevitably lose fine-grained local semantics crucial for anti-spoofing success. Current academic focus has irreversibly shifted toward Vision-Language Models prompt learning and Generative Diffusion Models. Whether by strongly binding ambiguous visual pixel artifacts to clear, definitive textual concepts (as in FLIP and CFPL-FAS), or by introducing environmental noise and reconstructing images to synthetically exhaust all possible attack styles (as in DiffFAS), modern liveness detection networks now possess powerful reasoning and anticipation capabilities. They no longer defend passively after an attack but synthetically exhaust future domain shift variables during the training phase itself.

Second, for high-specification financial and security physical access systems, deeply mining hard-to-forge deep physiological and multi-modal physical cues remains the most insurmountable security baseline. Although ultra-high-definition screens and high-refresh-rate panels can easily deceive the texture recognition modules of ordinary RGB cameras, they absolutely cannot replicate the rhythmic optical chroma variations (rPPG) caused by the heart pumping blood in subcutaneous capillaries of authentic human faces. Latest research, by introducing 4D Gaussian representations and explicit dual-disentanglement of motion and chroma, can now accurately strip away this extremely faint vital sign pulse wave even from severely degraded video streams suffering from drastic head shaking and strong environmental light interference. In the future, such detection methods grounded in molecular physics and biomedical features, combined with adaptive uncertainty filtering mechanisms for multi-sensor data under the MMDG framework, will serve as the absolute core against the highest tier of physical 3D mask attacks.

Finally, the comprehensive rollout of Unified Attack Detection (UAD) will be the ultimate goal over the next two to three years. Historically, Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) at the physical sensor frontend and digital Deepfake Detection at the backend have operated independently within system architectures. This not only caused severe redundancy in computational power but also left significant security vulnerabilities at various nodes of data transmission.
